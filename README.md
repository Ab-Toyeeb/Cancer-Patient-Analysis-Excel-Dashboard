# Hospital Cancer Treatment Outcomes Dashboard

![Cancer-Patient-Analysis-Excel-Dashboard](./Hospital%20Dashboard.png)

## 📋 Project Overview

This is a comprehensive **Excel Dashboard** that analyzes treatment outcomes for **5,000 cancer patients** across three major treatment types: **Chemotherapy**, **Radiation Therapy**, and **Surgery**.

The dashboard provides clear insights into patient status (Active, Deceased, In Remission), cancer types, age groups, disease stages, gender distribution, and geographic spread.

**Key Highlights:**
- Total Patients Analyzed: **5,000**
- Most common cancer: **Melanoma** (1,691 cases)
- Old Age group has the highest number of patients (1,654)
- Surgery treated the most patients (1,679)
- Highest remission rate: Radiation Therapy

---

## 🎯 Business / Analytical Objectives

- Compare effectiveness of Chemotherapy, Radiation, and Surgery
- Analyze patient outcomes by treatment type
- Identify patterns across cancer types, age groups, stages, and countries
- Support data-driven decisions in oncology and hospital management

---

## 📊 Key Insights

### Treatment Outcomes
| Treatment       | Total Patients | Active | Deceased | In Remission | Alive Rate |
|-----------------|----------------|--------|----------|--------------|------------|
| Chemotherapy    | 1,665          | 545    | 593      | 527          | 64.4%      |
| Radiation       | 1,656          | 533    | 550      | 573          | 66.8%      |
| Surgery         | 1,679          | 537    | 567      | 575          | 66.2%      |

### Other Findings
- **Cancer Types**: Melanoma (1,691), Retinoblastoma (1,672), Lymphoma (1,637)
- **Age Groups**: Old Age (1,654), Young Adult (1,137), Middle Age (1,060)
- **Top Countries**: South Africa (544), Australia (513), France (505)
- **Stages**: Fairly balanced distribution (Stage I–IV)
- **Gender**: Almost even split (Female 32.6%, Male 33.0%, Other 34.4%)
- Average Patient Age: **45 years**

**Note**: Chemotherapy shows the highest deceased count in this dataset (possibly due to more advanced cases being assigned to it). No causal conclusions should be drawn without clinical context.
